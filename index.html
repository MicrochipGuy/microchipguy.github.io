How I built a Bluetooth remote controlled car

Intro Current progress Disclaimer Prerequisites Special thanks

When I was 18, 19 years old, I began to build a small remote controlled car. At the very beginning, it was made of Lego Technic beams and axles, but time passed, and I kept upgrading it. The first upgrades were mostly design upgrades: front wheel drive, live rear axle (very useful to cross the small doorstep of my bedroom). But as I kept acquiring hardware, the car underwent deeper changes: control via Bluetooth with Arduino rather than the Lego radiofrequency system, 3D printed chassis, return to rear wheel drive, and rack steering.

In this series of articles, we will see together how I to build this radio controlled car in its current state, step by step, from zero.

Current progress

At the time of writing, the car is still quite simple. It features a modular chassis, made of 3D printed elements bolted together. It has a mid-engine, rear wheel drive design, with a live rear axle allowing it to cross small obstacles. The low ground clearance allowed by the motor and the differential limits obstacle size to a couple millimeters to half a centimeter (1/8 to 1/5 inch), however it should be enough to keep it stable while on interior use, or while crossing a bedroom doorstep, or moving on irregular tiled floor.

The car is moved by a Lego(-like) Power Functions small motor, linked to an Arduino Uno board. To achieve this, I had to cut a Lego Power Functions wire extender and wire it to a L293D dual H-bridge. You can find more about this here. The power is transmitted to the rear wheels using Lego Technic beams, through a 3D-printed open rear differential. As I am unable−as of today−to 3D print tyres, the car is also equipped with Lego Technic wheels.

Unfortunately, the older Lego Power Functions parts I used are no longer in production. Two solutions remains: buying used parts (but they will eventually run out of stock), or buying clones/compatible parts. I will keep you informed from my research.

At the heart of the car, we find an Arduino Uno board, wired to a micro-servomotor for steering, a L293D to control the main motor, a HC-05 Bluetooth module to communicate, and a 9V battery box (or just 9V battery, we will discuss on the difference later). Unfortunately, the Bluetooth module is not compatible with Apple devices.

Last thing is the app on the phone. Unfortunately, the HC-05 Bluetooth module is not compatible with Apple devices, an Android smartphone is required to play with the car. The app will be created using MIT App Inventor. Even though it is accessible for a bunch of people, it has a few downsides: it has poor to no error handling mechanisms, and I had to face some bugs once the app was finished. If I am motivated enough, I will show you how to code an app in Java with libGDX.

Disclaimer

The car described in this article is intended to be a toy, however it is not suitable for children under 4 to 6 years old (because of the small parts). An adult supervision may be desirable.

The car is primarily designed, and has been tested only for, interior use. Take it outside at your own risks.

Prerequisites

To build the car, you will need some hardware, software, and tools.

Let's first enumerate the needed electric and electronic stuff:

  • An Arduino Uno board
  • A computer running Arduino IDE
  • A Bluetooth HC-05 module
  • A SG-90 micro micro-servomotor
  • A 9V (6LR61/6HR61) battery or a 6× AA (R6/LR6) battery box
  • A 9V battery connector
  • A micro breadboard
  • A L293D integrated circuit
  • A Lego Power Functions or compatible extender wire
  • A Lego Power Functions or compatible small motor
  • A bunch of jumper wires and/or 22AWG electric wire

For the mechanical side you will need:

  • A 3D printer or a way to get the 3D printed parts
  • 4×16mm and 4×12mm bolts, 4mm nuts
  • Wrenches and screwdrivers

To run the app, you will need a device running Android 10 or higher.
